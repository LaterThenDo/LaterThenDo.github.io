History

Back in May 1882, Jigoro Kano took inspiration from traditional combat techniques and developed a new system that aimed to train not just the body, but also the mind and character. He called it judo.

At first, judo was mainly seen as a way to defend oneself. But over time, people began to realise its true value, not just as a martial art, but also as a powerful tool for physical and personal development.

Judo reached a global stage when it became an Olympic sport during the 1964 Tokyo Games. Today, the International Judo Federation (IJF) includes over 200 national federations and five continental unions.

Common Techniques

Osoto Gari
Osoto Gari is one of the most fundamental and powerful throws in judo. The name means "major outer reap" in Japanese. In this technique, the attacker (tori) steps beside their opponent (uke), usually pulling them slightly off balance toward the back. Then, the tori uses their to sweep the uke's leg from the outside while driving them backward to the ground. It is a clean, effective move that relies on timing, balance, and control, and is often one of the first throws beginners learn in judo.
Ouchi Gari
Ouchi Gari, which means "major inner reap" in Japanese, is a classic judo throw that targets the opponent's inner leg. In this technique, the attacker (tori) steps in close to the opponent (uke), usually pulling them slightly forward and to the side to break their balance. Then, the tori uses their own leg to sweep the uke's leg from the inside while pushing them backward, causing them to fall. It's a smooth and effective move, often used in combination with other throws to catch the opponent off guard.
Ippon Seoi Nage
Ippon Seoi Nage, which means "one-arm shoulder throw" in Japanese, is a fast and dynamic judo technique where the attacker (tori) throws the opponent (uke) over their shoulder. To execute it, the tori grips one of the uke's arms, turns in closely, and drops their body low while pulling the arm over their shoulder. With a strong lift and twist, the uke is flipped over and lands on their back. This throw is popular for its speed and impact, and when done cleanly, it can score an immediate ippon (full point) in a judo match.
Harai Goshi
Harai Goshi, which means "sweeping hip throw" in Japanese, is a powerful judo technique where the attacker (tori) uses their hip and leg to throw the opponent (uke) to the ground. The tori first pulls the uke forward to break their balance, then turns their body and places their hip across the uke's waist. At the same time, the tori sweeps the uke's leg upward and outward with their own leg, using the hip as a pivot point to flip the uke over. It is a strong and graceful throw, often used by more experienced judokas for its effectiveness and fluid motion.

Randori (Sparring)

Randori in judo is a form of free practice where two judokas spar in a controlled and respectful manner to apply techniques in a realistic setting. Unlike in formal matches, randori allows both players to move freely and attempt throws, holds, or submissions without a set outcome. The goal isn't to win, but to improve timing, balance, and technique under pressure.

There are different intensities and focuses in randori, sometimes it is more technical to refine form, while sometimes it is more competitive to help judokas test their techniques under pressure. Through regular randori, judokas learn to adapt to different opponents, react instinctively, and apply techniques more effectively in real situations.

There are 2 types of randori in Judo with each randori training a different area of your judo match.

  • Tachi-waza Randori (Standing Techniques)

    Tachi-waza randori focuses on grappling while both judoka are standing up on their feet. The goal is to simply control your opponent, break their balance, and execute clean throws and counters.

  • Ne-waza Randori (Ground Techniques)

    Ne-waza Randori on the other hand is grappling while both judoka are on the ground. Ne-waza Randori includes pins, chokes, and joint locks, depending on the rank and rules. It trains one's pressure and control, escapes from pins or chokes, and transitions from pins to submissions.

Mistakes are meant to be made during randori. What's important is to learn from them. Some common mistakes that beginners make during randori are the following:

Relying purely on strength

Using brute force will tire you out faster and develop bad habits that are ineffective against better or heavier opponents. You should focus on your technique, timing, and balance.

Repeating the same throw

This makes you predictable, and prevents you from growing and improving yourself, and also allows your opponent to counter a lot easier. Try a different throw or setup every randori session.

Not controlling grips

Letting your opponent control you is a big problem. They would be able to control you far more efficiently, leaving you vulnerable to even the smallest throws.

Competition

Judo competitions are matches where two judokas try and throw, pin, or submit each other to score points and win. The goal is to use controlled techniques to either score 1 full point (ippon), or by holding the opponent down for a certain amount of time. If no ippon is scored, the judoka with the most points at the end wins.

Matches are divided by weight and age categories to keep things fair. There are also time limits, which is usually 4 minutes. If the score is tied when time runs out, the match goes into golden score, a sudden death overtime where the first person to score wins. Judo competitions are not just about strength, they test balance, timing, technique, and strategy, all while following strict rules that promote safety, respect, and fair play.

  • Ippon (Full point)
    • The opponent lands flat on their back after a clean throw
    • The opponent was pinned to the ground 20 seconds
    • The opponent submits after an arm-lock or choke
  • Waza-ari (Half point)
    • The opponent was thrown, but did not land flat on their back.
    • The opponent was pinned to the ground 10 seconds
    • 2 waza-ari = ippon!
  • Shido (Penalty)
    • Acts as a warning for breaking the rules
    • 3 shidos = disqualification

A shido is called when:

  • A judoka stalls or runs away from their opponent
  • A judoka grabs their opponent's leg
  • A judoka is too defensive
  • A judoka uses illegal grips

Hansoku-make (Major Penalty)

A hansoku-make is called when a judoka gets 3 shidos and result in instant loss. But also can be called when:

  • A judoka does a dangerous throw
  • A judoka does an illegal technique
  • A judoka intentionally injures their opponent
  • A judoka fights dirty
